# STREHE Marketing Website Strategy

## Status

Draft for review before design or implementation.

This document defines the public website strategy for STREHE as a marketing and lead-generation website with login access to the internal app.

The goal is to decide the website properly before building it.

---

## 1. Website Role

The website is not the product.

The website exists to:

- explain the business clearly
- build trust
- attract qualified leads
- help property owners understand the service quickly
- provide a clear login entry for staff/admin users of the app

The website does not exist to:

- expose internal operational workflows
- act like a client portal in v1
- present the internal software as the thing being sold
- confuse visitors with experimental features

### Working Definition

`The public website is a trust-first marketing website for diaspora apartment owners who live abroad and own apartments in Prishtina or Fushë Kosovë.`

---

## 2. Business Context

The real business is:

- regular apartment visits and checks
- recurring apartment care while the owner is abroad
- practical follow-up with technicians or service providers when needed
- reporting with photos, updates, and clear communication

The internal app supports this work, but it is not the public offer.

The website should therefore feel like:

- a serious local service business
- professional
- premium-trustworthy
- calm
- organized

It should not feel like:

- a startup SaaS landing page
- an AI company
- a generic real estate portal
- a cheap maintenance classifieds page

---

## 3. Target Audience

### Primary Audience

The primary audience is not all property owners.

It is a narrow and valuable slice:

- diaspora apartment owners
- living abroad
- owning apartments in Prishtina or Fushë Kosovë
- wanting a trusted local person or team to look after the property while they are away

### Core customer profile

The best-fit launch customer is someone who:

- lives abroad
- owns one or more apartments in Prishtina or Fushë Kosovë
- is not in Kosovo most of the time
- wants to avoid depending on relatives, neighbors, or improvised help
- values trust and responsiveness over the cheapest option
- wants updates, structure, and local coordination without daily involvement

### Customer motivations

The customer is likely trying to solve one or more of these:

- “I do not know what is happening in the apartment while I am away.”
- “I do not want to ask family for favors every time.”
- “I want the apartment ready when I come back.”
- “I want problems caught early.”
- “I want one trustworthy contact in Kosovo.”

### Likely geographies

- Germany
- Switzerland
- Austria
- United Kingdom
- United States
- broader Europe

### Property type at launch

- apartments only

Houses are not part of the launch offer, though the website may collect interest for future expansion.

### Who this is not for at launch

The site should quietly make it clear that STREHE is not primarily for:

- local landlords living in Kosovo
- house owners at launch
- nationwide all-property support
- bargain shoppers looking for the cheapest one-off maintenance fix

That matters because sharper exclusion increases trust and improves lead quality.

### Secondary Audience

The emotional second audience may often be:

- the spouse or partner
- the family member who worries more about risk, cleanliness, readiness, and trust

This matters because the decision may not be made only by the nominal property owner.

In many cases, one person owns the apartment, but another person strongly influences whether they trust a company enough to hand over access and responsibility.

So the site should not be built only for:

- rational comparison

It should also support:

- emotional safety
- reassurance
- confidence

### Decision reality

The site should assume that the final decision may be influenced by two voices:

1. the owner asking: “Is this practical and worth it?”
2. the spouse or family voice asking: “Can we actually trust these people?”

Both need to feel answered.

## 6. Strategic Promise

The site should communicate one core promise:

`Your apartment is not left unattended while you are abroad.`

That promise should immediately imply:

- someone local is looking after it
- you get visibility
- problems are found early
- access and coordination are handled responsibly
- your apartment is ready when you need it

### What we are really selling

This is stronger than selling:

- inspections
- cleaning
- maintenance

because those are service categories.

The real thing being sold is:

- peace of mind
- trusted local control
- reduced uncertainty

### Concrete-language rule

The strategy and the final website copy should prefer visible actions over abstract service words.

Examples:

- “oversight” should usually become “we visit, check, and report”
- “coordination” should usually become “we contact, arrange, and follow up”
- “support” should usually become “we handle practical tasks and communication”

The visitor should be able to imagine what actually happens.

### Promise test

If a homepage sentence does not support one of these outcomes:

- trust
- visibility
- local control
- peace of mind

then it is probably not central enough to lead the site.

## 8. Emotional Strategy

The site should not aim only to persuade through features.

It should reduce three specific anxieties:

### Anxiety 1: "Who is actually checking my apartment?"

The site must make the company feel:

- real
- local
- reachable
- accountable

### Anxiety 2: "Can I trust them with access?"

The site must make the company feel:

- careful
- serious
- secure
- structured

### Anxiety 3: "Will I have to chase everything myself anyway?"

The site must make the service feel:

- organized
- responsive
- easy to work with
- relieving, not burdensome

### Important emotional note

You mentioned wanting it to feel more appealing to wives and more extravagant.

That instinct contains something useful, but it should be translated carefully.

The better version is not:

- flashy
- luxurious for its own sake

The better version is:

- elegant
- polished
- emotionally reassuring
- discreetly premium
- comfortable and trustworthy

So the emotional target should be:

`safe, beautiful, calm, competent`

not:

`showy, loud, glamorous`

That distinction matters a lot.

## 9. Trust Strategy

The website should build trust in layers.

### Layer 1: Clarity

Visitors should understand fast:

- what STREHE does
- where it operates
- who it is for
- what kind of properties it handles

### Layer 2: Specificity

Specificity increases trust.

Examples:

- apartments only at launch
- Prishtina and Fushë Kosovë
- owners living abroad
- recurring checks
- local coordination

### Layer 3: Professional structure

Visitors should feel:

- there is a process
- there is a standard
- this is not informal favor-based help

### Layer 3A: Trust mechanisms

The site should explain how trust works in practice.

At minimum, the public website should eventually show or describe:

- how apartment access and keys are handled
- what happens during a visit
- what the owner receives afterward
- who is responsible for follow-up

Examples of concrete trust mechanisms:

- visits are documented
- updates are sent after visits
- issues are reported clearly
- technician coordination is followed through, not just arranged once

Rule:

Trust should be shown through process and structure, not only adjectives.

### Layer 4: Human reassurance

The brand should feel:

- local
- human
- responsive

not faceless.

### Layer 5: Proof

At first, proof may be limited.

Possible trust elements:

- clear service process
- founder presence
- an example of what a visit update or report looks like
- testimonials later
- WhatsApp / phone / email availability
- clear region and specialization

### Trust must be mechanism, not adjectives

The website should not rely too heavily on words like:

- trusted
- reliable
- professional

unless it also shows how that trust works in practice.

The site should explain trust through mechanisms such as:

- how visits happen
- what is checked
- what the owner receives after a visit
- how issues are reported
- how access or keys are handled
- who remains responsible for follow-up

### Primary goals

1. Convert qualified visitors into leads
2. Make the business feel trustworthy
3. Make the offer easy to understand

### Secondary goals

1. Give people a reason to contact STREHE now
2. Give staff a clear login path
3. Create a polished public face for the business

At launch, the website should mainly generate:

- contact form leads
- WhatsApp inquiries
- phone inquiries

Optional later:

- consultation booking
- callback request

For launch, the primary conversion order should be:

1. WhatsApp
2. Contact form
3. Phone

Why:

- WhatsApp is likely the easiest trust bridge for diaspora users
- contact form is best for structured follow-up
- phone is important, but should not be the only main path

Primary CTA:

- Ask us a quick question on WhatsApp

Secondary CTA:

- Request Information

Support CTA:

- Staff Login

This gives the site a cleaner behavioral priority.

### CTA wording rule

CTA language should feel:

- easy
- warm
- low-risk

Better direction:

- Ask us a quick question on WhatsApp
- Request information
- We usually reply the same day

- try to explain every operational detail
- over-explain the software

- make houses feel equally supported at launch
- overpromise instant full-scale property management

The website should be disciplined.

## 15. Lead Capture Strategy

The site should not depend on one single contact method.

### Recommended primary lead paths

### Path 1: WhatsApp

Best for:

- fast trust
- diaspora convenience
- low-friction contact

### Path 2: Contact form

Best for:

- more structured lead capture
- internal follow-up discipline

### Path 3: Phone

Best for:

- urgent or high-trust conversations

### Minimum lead fields

Do not make the form too long.

Recommended:

- name
- email or phone
- do you live abroad?
- country where they live
- area of apartment
- short message

Optional later:

- property type
- number of apartments
- interest in house service later

### House lead capture

Because houses are not launch scope, the site can still include a soft mechanism:

- “Need support for a house instead? Tell us and we’ll let you know when that service opens.”

This is useful without pretending the service is already active.

### Recommended launch form stance

The contact form should collect enough to qualify the lead, but not enough to feel heavy.

The right balance is:

- useful for follow-up
- easy to complete

### Recommended follow-up posture

The site should make the first step feel like:

- a conversation

not:

- a service application

### Recommended qualification rule

The form should qualify without becoming heavy.

One especially useful qualifier at launch is:

- Do you live abroad?

- too technical

1. does it sound human?
2. does it reduce uncertainty?
3. does it still say something concrete?

## 19. Key Risks

### Risk 1: Too broad

If we speak to everyone, the site will feel weak.

Mitigation:

- target diaspora apartment owners abroad
- target Prishtina + Fushë Kosovë
- apartments first

### Risk 2: Too generic

If we sound like every maintenance company, trust drops.

Mitigation:

- lead with oversight and peace of mind
- use specificity

### Risk 3: Too flashy

If we try to look luxurious instead of trustworthy, credibility drops.

Mitigation:

- elegant, not flashy
- premium, not theatrical

### Risk 4: Too much text

If the site explains everything, people won’t read it.

Mitigation:

- clear page purpose
- strong hierarchy
- CTA visible early

- public pricing too early can create cheap-comparison behavior before enough brand trust exists

Better launch approach:

- invite contact
- explain service clearly
- discuss the right support level after inquiry

#### First CTA

Recommendation:

- WhatsApp first

This is considered decided unless a stronger reason appears later.

#### Login placement

Recommendation:

- top navigation
- small utility link in hero or header area

But it should remain clearly secondary to lead-generation actions.

#### House-interest capture

Recommendation:

- yes, but softly

Meaning:

- do not present houses as part of the launch offer
- do allow visitors to register interest for future support

#### Service-page framing

Recommendation:

- lead with recurring apartment oversight
- present practical support as supporting layers
- do not make the page feel like a generic maintenance menu

#### Renovation oversight at launch

Recommendation:

- mention it lightly as advanced support or coordination help
- do not make it dominate the launch service story

### Review-ready default position

If we paused decision-making here and moved into review, the current working defaults would be:

- voice: warm and reassuring, with practical discipline
- lead CTA: WhatsApp first
- pricing: not public at launch
- login: top navigation plus small utility link
- houses: interest capture only, not launch offer
- renovation oversight: supporting mention, not core lead service

That means the document is now strong enough for a proper review round even if we have not frozen every single choice forever.

## 24. Diaspora-Family Trust Visual Brief

This is the chosen visual direction for the public website.

The site should feel built for diaspora owners and the family members who influence trust, not for trend-driven startup branding.

### Core emotional goal

The design should help a visitor feel:

- this is safe
- this is serious
- this is respectful
- this is warm enough to trust
- this is polished enough to share with family

### Primary feeling

The public site should feel:

- calm
- trustworthy
- family-oriented
- respectful
- warm
- polished
- local but refined

### What the site should not feel like

The public site should not feel like:

- a tech startup
- a flashy luxury brand
- a cheap maintenance company
- an aggressive sales page
- a cold corporate website

### Design principle

The right question for each visual decision is:

`Would this make a family abroad feel more comfortable handing over responsibility for a home?`

If the answer is yes, the direction is probably right.

If the choice only makes the site look more expensive, dramatic, or trendy, it is not enough.

### Color direction

The palette should feel:

- deep
- grounded
- warm
- dignified

Recommended direction:

- deep charcoal or ink-like dark surfaces
- warm stone or soft neutral supporting surfaces
- restrained gold, brass, or muted warm accent tones used sparingly
- strong readable contrast for text and actions

Avoid:

- bright startup gradients
- playful color combinations
- overly glossy luxury palettes
- purple-heavy technology branding

### Typography direction

Typography should feel:

- clean
- modern
- composed
- readable

It should communicate:

- confidence
- clarity
- restraint

Avoid:

- decorative luxury type
- overly compressed or edgy typography
- anything that feels trendy before it feels trustworthy

### Image direction

Imagery should support trust first.

Recommended image direction:

- real apartments
- clean, cared-for interiors
- lived-in but orderly spaces
- warm natural light
- details that suggest readiness, cleanliness, and care
- occasional human presence only when it feels natural and grounded
- signs of active care, not just beautiful rooms

### Visual trust rule

The website should not only show clean apartments.

It should also show evidence of active care.

Helpful visual directions include:

- someone checking the apartment carefully
- notes, checklists, or practical follow-up moments
- keys or access being handled responsibly
- a small repair or preparation task in progress
- a phone or update moment that suggests reporting back

The imagery should imply:

- someone responsible is paying attention
- the home matters
- the property is being cared for respectfully

Avoid:

- generic office/business stock photos
- fake smiling team shots
- flashy luxury lifestyle clichés
- overly staged real-estate glamour imagery
- visuals that feel cold, empty, or transactional
- images that feel like an Airbnb listing or a hotel gallery

### Anti-gallery rule

The site should not feel like:

- a real-estate listing site
- an interior-design portfolio
- a luxury apartment gallery

It should feel like:

- a well-cared-for apartment
- a home being looked after responsibly
- an active service, not passive aesthetics

### Layout and spacing direction

The layout should feel:

- organized
- open
- calm
- easy to follow

Recommended direction:

- generous spacing
- strong section rhythm
- clear hierarchy
- uncluttered CTA placement
- enough breathing room for the site to feel respectful and composed

Avoid:

- dense landing-page stacking
- noisy card overload
- visually crowded trust sections
- too many competing highlights on one screen

### Component behavior

Buttons, cards, forms, and CTA areas should feel:

- clear
- steady
- calm
- reliable

They should not feel:

- flashy
- pushy
- hyper-promotional

Practical implications:

- primary CTAs should be prominent but not loud
- forms should feel simple and low-pressure
- trust sections should use clarity and structure more than decorative effects

### Family-shareability test

The public site should feel respectable enough that a visitor would comfortably send it to:

- a spouse
- a parent
- a sibling
- a relative helping with the decision

and feel that it reflects well on them.

That means the site should look:

- emotionally calm
- mature
- tasteful
- trustworthy

### Premium definition

For this website, premium does not mean:

- flashy
- glamorous
- ornamental

Premium means:

- structured
- reliable
- accountable
- visually composed
- carefully presented

### Working visual summary

If we compress the entire visual direction into one line, it is:

`Diaspora-family trust, expressed through calm structure, warm restraint, and respectful polish.`
